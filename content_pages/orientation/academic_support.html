Introduction to Research in Education

The ERIC (Education Resource Information Center) database is probably the single best starting point for research in education. ERIC is a huge bibliographic database that lists virtually everything published related to education. This is the place to go to search for sources related to a topic you want to research for a paper or your Master’s Project. You may or may not be able to access the actual source through ERIC if it is not public (e.g. a book or subscription-only journal), but this will help you find what’s out there and then you can actually access it through the NU Library using the methods below. The Education Abstracts and Education Administration Abstracts databases are also good bibliographic indexes. A “Find it at NU” button will often appear on the left-hand side when conducting a search in ERIC, Education Abstracts, or Education Administration Abstracts and the full-text articles are not available in the database you are using. Click on the icon, and it will lead you to the articles if NU subscribes to the publication. If NU does not have a subscription, you can request the article via Interlibrary Loan. For more information on “Find it at NU” watch the video tutorial.

General Introduction to the Library's Resources

Northwestern’s extensive library system on the Evanston and Chicago campuses provides resources and support for scholars engaged in research. Northwestern’s combined library collections rank among the top 35 in the nation. The online catalog is accessible from campus computers or off campus with VPN access.

Research Consultation

Li is the Librarian for the School of Education and Social Policy which includes the MSHE program. Through an individual research consultation, Li works with students to find useful books, articles, and dissertations for the Master's Project Sequence or other research papers. She can walk you through different search engines and help you to find sources you may not have come across during your own search.

To set up a consultation with Li:

  • Send an email to qunying-li@northwestern.edu
  • In the email, include your name and mention that you are a student in the MSHE program
  • Ask for an individual meeting, either in person at the Northwestern Library on the Evanston Campus, or a virtual meeting.
  • Propose some possible meeting days and times. Try to plan ahead and give some options when you are available in the next week or two.
  • Be sure to include your research topic, research question(s), and any search terms you have been using
  • Remember: the more specific you are in your e-mail about your research topic, the more prepared she will be to help you.

The Writing Place

The Writing Place is Northwestern’s center for free, peer writing consultations. Whether you are writing a paper for a class, composing application letters and essays, or working on some other writing project, a Writing Place consultant can help you at any stage of the writing process, from talking about ideas to developing a plan to revising and editing a draft.

  • Before making an appointment, you must register online, using your Northwestern e-mail address. That will become your Writing Place log-in. Register here
  • Make an appointment at one of the two locations for the Graduate Writing Place: Evanston Campus Graduate Writing Place: 555 Clark Street, Room 2-206 (second floor) or
  • Chicago Campus Graduate Writing Place: Abbott Hall (710 North Lakeshore Drive), room 121.
  • You can also set up an online appointment (via phone, Skype, or chat) if that is more convenient!
  • If you can't make it to your appointment, cancel it 12 hours in advance.
